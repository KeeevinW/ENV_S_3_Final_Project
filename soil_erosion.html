How Golf Courses Cause Soil Erosion

Intuitively, it is hard to connect golf courses with environmental damages because of how beautiful they are. However, if you look closely, their manicured fairways and sculpted landscapes often represent significant modifications to natural environments. As of 2018, it is reported that over 2 million acres of the United States are designated to golf courses. This large amount of land use must presented considerable erosion challenges.

During the construction of golf courses, vast areas are stripped of native vegetation to create fairways, greens, tees, and other course features (artificial lakes, bunkers, etc.). This phase exposes bare soil directly to the erosive forces of rainfalls and wind without protection.

Besides that, the intensive maintenance practices of golf courses also contribute to soil erosion. In pursuit of a fast green speed, heavy machinery such as greens rollers and mowers are often used to maintain the turf. These machines compact the soil, reducing its ability to absorb water and increasing the runoff rate. Frequent mowing keeps grass blades short, which correlates with less extensive root development. As a result, the shallow-rooted turf is less effective in anchoring the soil that natural vegetation, making it more vulnerable to being dislodged and transported by water or wind.

Another important factor is player traffic. Areas with concentrated foot traffic, such as tees, greens, and fairways if carts are allowed, are more prone to experience soil compaction, and eventually easier to erode.
