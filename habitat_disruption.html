How Golf Courses Influence Animal's Habitats

Golf courses are often linked with harmony between humans and the natural, where many animals can be found, such as deers, zebras, and even alligators. However, as previously mentioned, building a golf course requires significant modifications to the natural landscapes. These extensive land clearing can lead to deforestation and ecosystem disruptions, and hence the local wildlife.

The installation of golf courses frequently displaces native vegetation with non-native turfgrass species selected for play rather than ecological value. For local wildlife, this substitution eliminates their food sources. Furthermore, since golf courses uniform, manicured environment of fairways and greens, which contrasts sharply with the diverse, structurally complex habitats they replace, animals are forced to make way for new courses.

Beside this, to maintain their prestiine playing conditions, golf courses had to heavily rely on chemical inputs. In particular, pesticides, herbicides, and fertilizers are regularly applied to keep the grass green, free of pests and unwanted vegetation. Research indicates that over 50 pesticides are commonly used on golf courses, as a result of increasing vulnerability of grass to pests due to low heights.

These chemicals can be harmful to wildlife, as well as local ground water and aquatic ecosystems. When rain or irrigation water flows across treared areas, it can carry these chemicals into nearby streams, rivers, and lakes. Studies monitoring water sources near golf courses have identified 31 different pesticides in the water, indicating significant potential off-site contamination, which potentially harm fish, amphibians, and other water-dependent organisms. Furthermore, the persistance of some chemical compunds can lead to long-lasting environmental impacts.

The environmental consequence of extensive usage of chemical inputs on golf courses extend beyond the course boundaries. As fertilizers and pesticides seep into soil and groundwater, they can move throughout the hydrological cycle, potentially reaching drinking water sources and natural water bodies. This contamination can directly impact the aquatic life, disrupt food chains, and even pose risks to human health. (Source)

The storage of herbicides and pesticides also present additional risks. Most of the chemicals are stored in aboveground storage tanks. Leaks or spills from these tanks can result in concentrated sontamination events. Such contamination can cause acute environmental danages, requiring expensive remediation efforts.

To improve the situation of habitat disruption caused by golf courses, in January 2018, Audubon International and Environmental Defense Fund parthered to launch Monarchs in the Rough program, which guides golf courses in planting monarch butterfly, which has declined by about 90 percent over the past 20 years, habitats in out-of-play areas along the course. Till now, this program has helped more than 300 golf courses to build butterfly habitats.
